# Influence

Influence is the measure of how far a story travels when it touches you. It is not a title, a rank, or an assigned role — it is a current state of social prominence, earned and lost through lived experience, and amplified or suppressed by the people who carry your name through the world.

---

## Core Principle

Most people are never talked about beyond those who actually knew them. The few who become prominent are usually the ones who happened to be in the right — or wrong — place at the right time, brushing against someone or something that made the world take notice. This is not a flaw in the simulation. It is the point.

Influence is rare. When it emerges, it should feel earned, accidental, or unjust — because that is how it works in life.

---

## The Aggregate and the Named

Every person begins in the **aggregate** — the undifferentiated mass of villagers, travellers, craftspeople, and farmers that the simulation tracks as population. They have traits, they age, they consume resources, they live and die.

A character **crystallises** out of the aggregate when their prominence score crosses a threshold. The simulation now tracks them by name. They become a named entity: someone the world talks about, reacts to, remembers.

Named status is not permanent. Prominence decays without events to sustain it. The lord's son who never leaves the manor drifts back. The merchant whose reputation was built on a single good year fades when the years that follow are quiet. Named status is a current condition, not a permanent elevation.

---

## How Prominence Moves

Prominence rises through **narrative traits** — labels attached to a character by the event system as a consequence of actions taken.

Examples: `local hero`, `oath-breaker`, `famine survivor`, `peacemaker`, `notorious thief`, `touched by loss`

A single narrative trait is rarely enough. They accumulate. Three small acts of public courage may push a peasant across the threshold. One act of spectacular cowardice may erase years of accumulated standing.

Prominence also has a **heredity modifier**. The blacksmith's son starts higher than a foundling. The lord's daughter starts higher still. This is not destiny — it is probability. A lord's child who does nothing exceptional will still fade. A foundling who happens to be present when history moves may rise further than any noble ever did.

**Prominence decays naturally** with time and inaction. A quiet life, however good, does not sustain named status. This is intentional: the simulation should produce mostly unremarkable lives, with emergence as the exception.

---

## Influence as Signal Amplifier

When a story involves a high-influence character, it travels further. The gossip chain that would ordinarily die at the second or third link breaks less often when the subject — or the witness — is someone people already know and care about.

Tom stealing a loaf of bread from a stranger goes nowhere. Tom stealing a loaf of bread from Bob — and Bob having enough influence that people in a tavern three villages over recognise his name — is what turns a hungry boy into a named entity. Tom did not earn his infamy. The social network did it for him, because he was unlucky enough to cross someone whose name carried weight.

Influence does not generate stories. It amplifies them.

---

## Ascent and Descent: the Same Mechanism

There is no separate system for rising and falling. Both are the same prominence score moving in response to events and their propagation. A character can descend by:

- Accumulating negative narrative traits (`coward`, `oath-breaker`, `petty thief`)
- Having positive traits stripped by counter-events
- Simply doing nothing that generates any trait at all

The simulation should produce villains and heroes with equal indifference. It has no preference. It only responds to what happens and who witnesses it.
